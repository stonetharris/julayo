3-Stage Project in Akure, Nigeria

Akure, Nigeria has >800,000 inhabitants and a catchment area of >1.5 million people who are all served by one public hospital. In Akure, people suffer from preventable and easily treatable diseases.

Stage 1: Primary Care

  • ○ Primary care clinic and vaccination center.
  • ○ Adult primary care, pediatrics, and gynecology.
  • ○ Focus will be on preventative medicine (i.e. diabetes and cancer screenings) and health maintenance (i.e. blood pressure management) in addition to as well-child care.
  • ○ Health education outreach program.
  • ○ The staff will be fully Nigerian providing an opportunity for employment and community development.
  • ○ We have access to a 6 bedroom flat in a central location which we plan to use as our initial clinic. Currently, we are in the process of getting permits and starting to renovate this space.

Stage 2: School of medical sciences

  • ○ Training programs for nursing, emergency medical technicians, pharmacy technicians, radiology technicians, medical assistants, medical secretaries and health educators.
  • ○ Clinical clerkships will be held at the primary care clinic where appropriate.
  • ○ The professors will come from both Nigeria and overseas.
  • ○ Tuition will be at market rate with an opportunity for scholarships as funding allows.

Stage 3: Medical Center

  • ○ 75 bed hospital with full operating room suite and labor and delivery unit. Subspecialties included:
  • → Orthopedics
  • → Infectious disease
  • → Ob/gyn
  • → Pediatrics
  • → Infectious disease
  • → General surgery
  • ○ There will be a bus providing free transportation to the hospital from the surrounding communities.

Why fund our project?

  • Julayo is committed to improving the health of those most in need by developing health care centers in underserved areas that are staffed and run by local people. The goal is to reverse brain drain, improve the local economy and supply much needed health care. We do this by setting up a primary care clinic and then a medical training center followed by a hospital when the community has enough training and resources to proceed.
